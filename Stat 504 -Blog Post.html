Breaking the Cycle: What 17 Years of U.S. Data Reveal About Teenage Pregnancy

Richel Ohenewaa Attafuah

4 min read
·
Just now

An eye-opening analysis of teenage birth rates across American counties — told through data, maps, and trends.

Introduction

In 2007, over 430,000 babies were born to teenage mothers in the U.S. By 2020, that number had dropped by more than half. But who’s been left behind?

Teenage pregnancy has long been more than a statistic — it’s a story of disrupted education, limited opportunities, and generational consequences. But what if we could break that cycle with the right insights? What if the data could reveal where we’re making progress and where we still have work to do?

As a data scientist passionate about social impact, I wanted to understand how far we’ve come — and how far we still have to go. In this project, I dive into county-level teenage birth rates in the U.S. from 2003 to 2020. By applying exploratory data analysis (EDA) techniques and crafting rich, colorblind-friendly visualizations, I uncover:

  • Long-term national and regional trends
  • The counties and states that lead — and lag
  • Patterns in data uncertainty that deserve closer attention

Whether you’re a policymaker, public health advocate, or data enthusiast, this visual journey offers both insights and impact.

The Dataset

The dataset comes from the National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS) and includes:

  • Teen birth rates per 1,000 females aged 15–19
  • 95% Bayesian credible intervals
  • County, state, and FIPS codes

All data span the years 2003 to 2020.

National Trends

Over the 17-year period, the U.S. has seen a steady decline in average teen birth rates. This trend reflects national progress and is one of the most optimistic insights of this analysis.

A sharp decline begins post-2010, possibly due to stronger health education, contraception access, and cultural shifts.

Geographic Distribution

To understand where teenage pregnancy is most and least prevalent, we examine regional and local variations.

High rates dominate counties in the South and Southwest. Lower rates appear in the Northeast and West Coast.

The faceted view reveals significant spatial decline in birth rates, with high-rate counties shrinking over time.

State-Level Insights

Some states consistently outperform others in reducing teen pregnancies. Here are the top 10 states:

Northeastern states like Massachusetts, New Hampshire, and New York lead in maintaining low rates.

And the bottom 10:

Southern states including Mississippi and Arkansas remain at the top — highlighting persistent regional disparities.

County-Level Extremes

Drilling deeper, we examine the counties with the highest and lowest teen birth rates.

These local extremes provide actionable insight for county-level interventions.

Understanding Uncertainty: Confidence Intervals

A unique part of this dataset is its credible intervals, which tell us how precise the birth rate estimates are.

Alaska and Nebraska show high variability, reminding us that sparse population or data quality issues can affect reliability.

Correlation Analysis

We explore how different variables relate:

  • Teen birth rate has a strong positive correlation with its upper and lower confidence bounds.
  • Negative correlation with year, confirming the downward trend over time.

These correlations validate observed trends and help in interpreting long-term shifts.

Closing Thoughts

This project demonstrates that teenage pregnancy in the U.S. has declined steadily, but regional and local disparities persist. Some states and counties have made outstanding progress, while others remain stagnant.

This analysis offers a valuable lens into:

  • Where further intervention is needed
  • How data precision varies across space
  • The success of national-level efforts to reduce teenage pregnancy

Final Words

Data alone doesn’t create change — people do. But data can guide us. It can spotlight the counties that need more resources, the states that are succeeding, and the years where real progress was made.

If you believe data can drive better policies and healthier futures, share this post. Let’s keep this conversation alive — and grounded in evidence.
